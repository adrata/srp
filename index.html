Long Duration Energy Storage

Due Diligence Framework for Evaluating Technology Providers

Framework Overview

This framework provides a structured, phased approach to evaluating long duration energy storage (LDES) technology providers. LDES systems are defined as storage capable of delivering electricity for 4+ hours, with many technologies targeting 10-100+ hours of duration. Each phase serves as a gate, allowing for progressive investment of time and resources only as confidence in the technology and provider increases.

Guiding Principles

  • Progressive engagement - invest more resources only as confidence grows
  • Independent verification - validate claims through third parties and site visits
  • Technology-appropriate assessment - different LDES types have different maturity levels
  • Commercial viability focus - technical promise must translate to deliverable products

1

Initial Screening

Goal: Quickly assess whether the opportunity warrants further investigation. This phase should require minimal time investment and filter out non-credible pitches early. Target: 1-2 hours of engagement.

Technology Fundamentals

  • What is your underlying energy storage mechanism - electrochemical, mechanical, thermal, or chemical?
  • What is your round-trip efficiency, and under what conditions was it measured?
  • What is your target storage duration in hours and power capacity in MW?
  • What is your expected system lifespan in years, and how many cycles can it perform?
  • What is your expected degradation profile over the system lifetime?

Team & Company Background

  • When was your company founded?
  • What relevant technical background does your founding team have in energy storage?
  • Who are your investors, and do they include energy-focused VCs or strategic partners?
  • What is your total funding to date and most recent round?
  • Do you have in-house engineering and manufacturing capability, or do you outsource?

Current Development State

  • What is your current Technology Readiness Level (TRL 1-9)?
  • Have you built working prototypes, and at what scale (kW or MW)?
  • Do you have any existing customers, pilots, or demonstration deployments?
  • What third-party validation have you received from national labs, universities, or consultants?
  • Are there peer-reviewed publications on your core technology?
Red Flags at This Stage
  • Claims that violate basic physics or thermodynamics (e.g., >100% efficiency)
  • Unwillingness to explain the core technology mechanism, even at a high level
  • No technical founders or domain experts on team
  • Vague or evasive answers about current development status
  • Claims of performance far exceeding all alternatives without scientific explanation
  • Pressure tactics or discouraging questions about technical details
  • Cannot provide any independent validation or testing references
Gate Criteria to Advance
  • Technology mechanism is scientifically plausible and explainable
  • Team has relevant technical credibility and track record
  • Claims are specific, measurable, and internally consistent
  • Evidence of tangible progress beyond concepts (working hardware)
  • No fundamental red flags identified
2

Technical Validation

Goal: Conduct deeper technical evaluation to validate core claims and understand technology maturity, risks, and development pathway. This phase may require NDA execution and more significant time investment.

Performance Validation

  • Can you provide raw test data from your prototype operations under NDA?
  • Under what specific conditions (temperature, humidity, load profiles) were your performance metrics measured?
  • How does your system's performance vary across different operating conditions?
  • What is your measured degradation rate, and how many cycles have you tested?
  • Have you conducted accelerated life testing? What methodology did you use?
  • What is your demonstrated round-trip efficiency versus your projected efficiency?

System Architecture & Safety

  • What are your major system components, and who are your suppliers?
  • Which components are proprietary versus commercially available off-the-shelf?
  • What are your key technical risks, and how are you mitigating them?
  • What failure modes have you identified and designed against?
  • What is your thermal management approach and fire safety design?
  • What safety certifications (UL, NFPA, etc.) have you achieved or are you pursuing?

Materials & Supply Chain

  • What are your critical raw materials?
  • Are any of your materials supply-constrained, geopolitically sensitive, or price-volatile?
  • What is your recyclability or end-of-life disposal plan?
  • Does your system contain hazardous materials requiring special handling or permits?
  • Do you have any single-source suppliers that represent concentration risk?

Independent Verification

  • Has a national lab (NREL, Sandia, PNNL) or independent facility validated your claims?
  • Are there published peer-reviewed papers on your technology?
  • Would you support us commissioning an independent technical review?
  • What standards compliance (IEEE, IEC, UL) have you achieved?
  • Have you participated in any DOE programs or received government funding?
Industry Best Practice

Per DOE guidance, LDES technologies should demonstrate TRL 8+ for commercial deployment. TRL 8 requires "actual system completed and qualified through test and demonstration." For emerging technologies (TRL 6-7), pilot projects with appropriate risk allocation may be appropriate.

Red Flags at This Stage
  • Refusal to share test data even under NDA
  • Test conditions don't reflect real-world utility operating environments
  • Critical components exist only in theory or early R&D
  • No clear explanation for how lab results will scale to commercial size
  • Reliance on materials with known supply constraints or cost volatility
  • Large gap between demonstrated and projected performance
  • No independent validation despite years of development
Gate Criteria to Advance
  • Core performance claims are supported by credible, verifiable data
  • Technical risks are identified and have documented mitigation plans
  • No fundamental barriers to scaling have been identified
  • Independent validation exists or can be arranged
  • Safety approach is credible and certifications are on track
3

Commercial & Financial Assessment

Goal: Evaluate the commercial viability, manufacturing readiness, and financial health of the provider to assess delivery and counterparty risk.

Cost & Economics

  • What is your current installed cost per kWh and per kW at your current scale?
  • What is your projected cost at commercial scale, and what assumptions drive that?
  • Can you provide a detailed cost breakdown: materials, manufacturing, BOS, installation?
  • What is your levelized cost of storage (LCOS) under realistic operating assumptions?
  • How do your economics change with different utilization profiles?
  • How does your cost compare to the DOE target of $0.05/kWh for LDES?

Manufacturing Readiness

  • Where and how will your systems be manufactured - in-house, contract, or partners?
  • What is your current manufacturing capacity in MW/year, and what is your expansion plan?
  • What is your realistic lead time from order to commissioning?
  • What quality control and testing processes do you have in place?
  • Are there any single points of failure in your manufacturing supply chain?
  • What is your Manufacturing Readiness Level (MRL)?

Company Financial Health

  • What is your current cash runway and monthly burn rate?
  • What is your plan and timeline for your next funding round?
  • Who are your major shareholders, and can you describe your cap table structure?
  • Are there any pending legal issues, IP disputes, or regulatory concerns?
  • What happens to warranties and support obligations if your company fails?
  • Is there parent company backing or can you provide financial guarantees?

Warranty & Support Structure

  • What commercial warranty (defect/workmanship) do you offer, and for how long?
  • What performance warranty do you offer for capacity, efficiency, and availability?
  • What is your O&M model, and what are expected annual costs as a percentage of capex?
  • What is your guaranteed response time for service issues?
  • Is remote monitoring and diagnostics included in your offering?
  • What spare parts availability and pricing commitments can you provide?
Key Contract Protections to Negotiate
  • Performance guarantees with liquidated damages for underperformance
  • Capacity degradation limits with buy-down or replacement remedies
  • Milestone payments tied to FAT (Factory Acceptance Test) and SAT (Site Acceptance Test)
  • Delay penalties (typically 1% per week, capped at 10-15%)
  • Parent company guarantee or letter of credit for warranty obligations
  • Technology escrow for critical IP in case of company failure
  • Serial defect warranty provisions
Red Flags at This Stage
  • Cost projections rely on unproven manufacturing breakthroughs or "learning curve" assumptions
  • No clear, funded path from current state to volume manufacturing
  • Company has less than 18 months runway with no committed funding path
  • Warranties that seem too good to be true or are vaguely defined
  • Unable or unwilling to provide detailed cost breakdowns
  • Resistance to standard contract protections (LDs, guarantees, escrow)
  • Cap table issues or investor conflicts that could affect operations
Gate Criteria to Advance
  • Economics are competitive or have credible path to competitiveness
  • Manufacturing plan is credible and adequately funded
  • Company has adequate financial runway or committed funding
  • Warranty and support model is reasonable and appropriately backed
  • Acceptable contract terms can be negotiated
4

Reference Verification

Goal: Validate claims through direct verification with existing customers, site visits, and third-party expert assessment. This is critical for emerging technology providers.

Customer References

  • Can we speak directly with your existing customers or pilot hosts without you present?
  • Can you provide contact information for at least three reference customers?
  • What has been your actual performance versus contracted performance at existing sites?
  • What technical issues have arisen at deployments, and how did you resolve them?
  • Were there any schedule delays on past projects? How were they handled?

Site Visits

  • Can we visit one of your operating installations and observe live operations?
  • Can we tour your manufacturing facilities and see your production processes?
  • Can we observe your system under various operating conditions including charge/discharge cycles?
  • Can we review real operational data directly from your monitoring system?
  • Can we speak with your on-site operations and maintenance personnel?

Independent Expert Assessment

  • What do independent industry analysts say about your technology and company?
  • Would you support us engaging a third-party Independent Engineer for technical review?
  • What is the broader scientific community's view of your technology approach?
  • How does your technology compare to competing LDES solutions in maturity and cost?
  • What do national lab researchers say about your technology category?
Industry Best Practice

For any significant procurement, engage an Independent Engineer (IE) firm with LDES experience to conduct technical due diligence. Firms like DNV, Black & Veatch, Sargent & Lundy, and others specialize in storage system assessment.

Red Flags at This Stage
  • No referenceable customers despite claims of deployments
  • Existing customers report significant performance gaps vs. promises
  • Unable or unwilling to allow unescorted customer conversations
  • Site visit requests repeatedly delayed or denied
  • Operating systems not performing to specification
  • Pattern of over-promising and under-delivering
  • References all have equity relationships with the provider
Gate Criteria to Advance
  • Customer references are positive, credible, and independent
  • Site visits confirm technology performs as claimed
  • No pattern of overpromising or delivery failures identified
  • Third-party experts validate the technology approach
  • Competitive assessment confirms this is a reasonable choice
5

Final Evaluation & Decision

Goal: Synthesize all findings, assess overall risk, and make a structured decision on whether and how to proceed with engagement.

Risk Assessment Summary (Internal)

  • What are the top 3-5 risks we have identified and their potential impacts?
  • What mitigations can we build into contract terms?
  • What is this provider's demonstrated track record of meeting commitments?
  • What is our financial exposure if the provider fails or technology underperforms?
  • What is the reputational risk if this project fails publicly?
  • Are the risks appropriate given the potential benefits and strategic value?

Strategic Fit (Internal)

  • How does this technology fit our specific grid needs and use cases?
  • What is the integration complexity with our existing infrastructure?
  • Are there regulatory, permitting, or interconnection considerations we need to address?
  • How does this compare to alternative LDES solutions we have evaluated?
  • Does this align with our decarbonization timeline and IRP commitments?
  • What is the strategic value of early adoption versus waiting for technology maturation?

Engagement Structure Options (Internal)

  • Should we start with a small pilot or proceed to larger deployment?
  • What contractual protections are essential versus nice-to-have?
  • What performance guarantees and remedies are appropriate for this risk level?
  • Should we consider co-development, equity investment, or offtake agreement?
  • What ongoing monitoring, reporting, and governance structure do we need?
  • What are the appropriate milestone gates for larger follow-on deployment?
Decision Framework
  • Proceed with Pilot: Technology promising but unproven at utility scale; limit initial exposure; define success criteria for scale-up
  • Proceed with Deployment: High confidence in technology and provider; acceptable risk profile; appropriate contract protections in place
  • Continue Monitoring: Interesting but not ready; specific milestones to trigger re-evaluation in 6-12 months
  • Strategic Partnership: High potential but needs support; consider co-development, demonstration hosting, or strategic investment
  • Decline: Fundamental concerns identified or better alternatives available; document reasons for future reference
Recommended Contract Structure for Emerging Technologies
  • Phased deployment with performance gates before scale-up
  • Strong performance guarantees with meaningful liquidated damages
  • Extended warranty periods (15-20 years) with capacity guarantees
  • Parent guarantee or LC backing warranty obligations
  • Technology escrow and step-in rights
  • Clear acceptance testing protocols (FAT/SAT)
  • Regular performance reporting and audit rights
  • Defined change order and dispute resolution processes
